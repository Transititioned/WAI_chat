# The Return of the All-Rounder (and Why We Need It)
## Part 1: The Context Article

Most of my articles are delivery-focused — how to make projects better, how to keep governance moving at speed, how to lift release management for DevOps, how to get real-world outcomes. They’re practical and immediate, full of things you can try straight away.

This one’s different.
It’s a thought piece — a look at the terrain before we start giving out the maps.

I’m lining up some of the recurring problems I’ve seen across organisations and roles. And because this is WorkFriend, the rest of the series will go deep on solutions, templates, and experiments you can run in your own context. But first, we need to name the pattern.

## Why the All-Rounder Is Back in the Conversation

Work inside large organisations has changed dramatically. Expectations are higher, timelines shorter, and information moves with far more momentum — especially now that AI accelerates decision cycles.

What hasn’t changed nearly as much is the organisational scaffolding around that work. Most structures still assume problems stay neatly inside teams or roles. When the pace increases but the structure doesn’t flex, the gaps start to show. The people who bridge those gaps are the all-rounders.

Every organisation has a few.
They’re the ones who understand how work joins together.
They can translate across disciplines.
They steady projects that would otherwise drift.

These aren’t “generalists in the old sense.” They’re people who operate in the spaces the formal structure doesn’t cover — not because they’re bypassing it, but because modern work doesn’t fit neatly inside the lines.

## When Good Frameworks Meet Stubborn Structures

Movements like Lean and Agile were built on good ideas. They encouraged flow, collaboration, and iterative decision-making. But both depended on something organisations rarely delivered: structural change.

Ceremonies were adopted without adjusting authority or bandwidth.
Processes moved faster, but upstream approvals didn’t.
Roles stayed narrow while the work widened around them.

Over time, more and more of the “holding together” work fell to the people with the broadest context — the all-rounders — simply because they were the only ones who could see across the whole picture.

## Where Things Quietly Go Off-Track

Projects can look healthy on paper while drifting badly underneath. Misalignments slip in between teams. A dependency gets missed. A design assumption changes in one area and no one realises the impact somewhere else.

This gap work — the alignment, translation, sense-making — usually lands on all-rounders. They’re the ones people turn to when something doesn’t fit the formal model but still needs to be resolved quickly.

The challenge is that this work often falls outside the neat boxes that governance and risk teams rely on. Decisions get made informally because the formal pathways are too slow for real delivery. Nothing malicious. Just the system showing its seams.

Organisations benefit enormously from this flexibility, but it sits uneasily inside the formal model. That tension is part of why all-rounders burn out or disappear without recognition of the load they were carrying.

## The Quiet Problem Behind All of This: No People-Contingency

Most organisations run with almost no slack in their people model. Calendars are full, teams are stretched, and capacity planning assumes everything will go to plan.

So when new dependencies emerge — and they always do — there’s nowhere for the work to land. All-rounders end up taking it on because they have the context and the trust to move between spaces without causing disruption.

It shows up clearly in real programmes.

*Years ago, I worked on an ERP program linked to a distribution centre build. On paper, everything looked stable. In reality, site engineering, HVAC load, rack design, POS placement, and logistics events were evolving in ways the ERP data model couldn’t support. No one had the horizontal view. I worked with the WMS functional consultant — the real hero — translating between operations, engineering, the ERP vendor, and IT to stop the whole thing veering off. I could do that only because my boss had protected my time instead of scattering me across multiple initiatives. Without that people-contingency, the cross-domain work simply wouldn’t have happened.*

Organisations often don’t realise how much capability is lost when all-rounders are thinly allocated or constantly bounced between projects.

## So What Does an All-Rounder Actually Do?

It’s not about doing “a bit of everything.”
It’s not heroics or firefighting.

A modern all-rounder:

- keeps the story of the work coherent,
- connects decisions across teams,
- makes dependencies visible before they hit,
- translates between domains,
- carries the memory of past decisions, and
- stitches together work that was never designed to join cleanly.

They operate in the connective tissue — the part of the system that holds shape when everything else is in motion.

## The Punchline

The return of the all-rounder isn’t nostalgia or a plea for generalists.
It’s a structural response to the pace and complexity of modern work.

But it comes with friction:

- structures still assume work is vertical,
- governance still dislikes ambiguity,
- specialists still run at full load,
- and organisations rarely fund any contingency.

This leaves all-rounders as the quiet back-pocket operators — leaned on heavily, but not always recognised for the coherence they create.
