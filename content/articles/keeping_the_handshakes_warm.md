# Keeping the Handshakes Warm When Taking Over a Client Project

When a vendor PM takes over a client project, the job isn’t just delivery — it’s diplomacy.  
The client’s watching, the team’s watching, and somewhere nearby, the Account Manager is quietly watching hardest of all.

---

## 1. The Hand You’re Actually Shaking

When you inherit a client project, you’re not just picking up tasks — you’re stepping into **someone else’s trust account**.

And hovering nearby, there’s always an Account Manager. You know the one — the *“don’t screw it up”* guy.

He’s the gatekeeper. He built the relationship, knows every promise made over coffee or corridor chat, and carries the memory of every small bruise the client ever took. Ignore him, and you’ll find doors start to close — quietly, politely, and permanently.

So before you fix a single Gantt chart, go find him. Ask what’s still tender, what’s already forgiven, and what must never be mentioned again.  
That conversation will save you weeks of political archaeology.

---

## 2. The Three Handshakes You Need to Keep Warm

A takeover is never just one handover. It’s three happening at once:

| Handshake | What It Needs | What to Avoid |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Client** | Calm and respect — they need to feel continuity. | Overpromising or changing tone too soon. |
| **Team** | Direction and air cover. | Blaming the last PM — it kills morale. |
| **Account Manager** | Alignment, reassurance, proof you’re not a risk. | Acting like you now own the relationship. |

Get those three right and you’ll feel the project **exhale**.

---

## 3. The Listening Tour

Your first week isn’t about proving competence — it’s about curiosity.

Do short one‑on‑ones with each key client contact. Ask:

- “What’s working for you?”
- “What’s been frustrating?”
- “If we could change one thing this month, what would it be?”

Then — **debrief your Account Manager immediately afterward**.  
They’ll tell you what landed, what didn’t, and which phrases mean *“not yet”* in that client’s dialect.

These 30‑minute conversations rebuild situational awareness without making noise.

---

## 4. Keep the Story Straight

To the client, the project story has two narrators:  
**you and the Account Manager**.

If those stories drift out of sync, the relationship unravels.

Before sending any update:

- Check your metrics and phrasing match how the AM reports upstairs.
- Don’t reopen old wounds under the guise of “clarification.”
- Avoid introducing new language for old issues — it sounds like you’re rewriting history.

For the first few weeks, CC the AM.  
Let them see how you communicate.  
Once trust settles, you can both lighten the CC list — but the rule never changes: **no surprises**.

---

## 5. Reset the Relationship, Not the Contract

You’ll want to rebuild artefacts — plans, budgets, registers.  
Good. Do it.

But before rewriting anything, find a **small promise** you can deliver quickly.

Maybe it’s clearing a long‑stalled action or producing a one‑page project map that finally makes sense. Deliver it quietly and let the AM be the one to tell the client first.

That’s how you buy credibility without calling it a “reset meeting.”

---

## 6. Pet the Gatekeeper (Genuinely)

Yes, the AM protects revenue — but they’re also protecting **you**.

They know what renewals, audits, and procurement cycles are coming.  
Ask what’s keeping them up at night.

When they see you looking after delivery *and* commercial health, you stop being a risk and start being an asset. That’s when future work arrives as a quiet message:

> **“Hey, you free next quarter?”**

---

## 7. Close the Loop Gracefully

If you do this well, the best compliment won’t come from the client.  
It’ll come from the AM:

> **“They trust us again.”**

That’s the real win —  
the handshakes stayed warm, the people take your calls,  
and the next project starts before this one ends.
