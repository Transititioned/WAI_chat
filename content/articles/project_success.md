# So… What Would Project Success Look Like?

You can tell when a project’s in trouble even before the metrics catch up.
Everyone’s “green,” the schedule’s still intact — but people have gone quiet.
You feel it in the air.

Still, quiet isn’t the end — it’s the signal that the project’s ready for top up, a cup of coffee and some snacks on the journey.

The old iron triangle (time, cost, scope) measures control, not impact.  
Modern thinkers like Antonio Nieto-Rodriguez, Harold Kerzner, Edoardo Favari, and Jeremy Nicholls have all evolved their vision of success toward moving the dial for the organisation.

So how do we know what our elevator pitch is — and which direction we should get the team rowing in?

## 1️⃣ Start by Asking Better Questions

Success is often a matter of perspective — and sometimes even debate.  
It looks different depending on where you’re standing.

So before you inherit a project or kick one off, spend an hour asking questions that make people think out loud:

| Purpose | Example Questions | Why It Matters |
|--------|-------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Clarify the “Why” | “What outcome will prove this was worth doing?” · “Who actually benefits if we pull this off?” | Forces people to define benefits, not deliverables. |
| Expose Assumptions | “What problem are we solving — and how do we know it exists?” | Surfaces wish‑lists posing as strategy. |
| Test Alignment | “If we had to trade cost, scope, or schedule — which would you protect first?” | Reveals the real hierarchy of priorities. |
| Measure What Counts | “What evidence will tell us later that value was created?” | Starts the benefits-realisation story before kickoff. |

These aren’t checklist questions — they’re listening tools.  
Pause after each one. Notice who answers first, who defers to whom, and who stays silent.  

And once people realise someone’s finally listening, the answers start to fall out.  
The business always knows what needs fixing — they’re just busy doing their day jobs.

> **WorkFriend Tip:** If the sponsor and delivery lead give different answers, the bridge might be out. Time to slow down the train.

## 2️⃣ Map the Real Decision Makers (Not the Org Chart)

Every org has two structures:  
the official one… and the one people actually use.

Identify the three layers:

- **Vision‑Setters** — define why the project matters  
- **Value Translators** — turn intent into daily actions  
- **Gatekeepers** — quiet blockers who can kill momentum with a single raised eyebrow  

Actually draw it.  
Who influences whom?  
What currency do they trade — reputation, risk tolerance, resources?

If each node can see how the project supports *their* version of success, you’ve built alignment the charter alone can never produce.

> **Field Test:** When meetings drift, ask: *“Whose success are we protecting here?”*  
It resets the room instantly.

## 3️⃣ Negotiate a Working Definition of Success

People often think they agree — until it’s time to sign.  
That’s why you need a **working contract**, not just a mission statement.

Write it on one page:

- **Purpose:** Why we’re doing this  
- **Boundaries:** What’s in, what’s out  
- **Trade‑offs:** How we decide when constraints clash  
- **Validation:** How (and by whom) success will be measured  

Run a 15‑minute calibration workshop with your sponsor.  
It beats six months of re-work — or a polite “so what?” at the launch party.

> **WorkFriend Summary:** Define success in a way you’ll still believe when the dust settles.

## 4️⃣ Evidence the Consensus

Opinions multiply faster than facts.  
The cure is **visible logic**.

- Use a **Project Canvas** to visualise benefits and assumptions  
- Maintain **line‑of‑sight**:  
  `Task → Output → Benefit → Strategic Objective → Purpose`  
- Translate value through Favari’s *management‑for‑stakeholders* lens:  
  Show how each group wins  

Consensus isn’t universal agreement — it’s the negotiated peace everyone can live with.

> **WorkFriend framing:**  
> “Here’s what we can prove.  
> Here’s what we’re betting on.  
> Here’s what we’ll check after go-live.”

## 5️⃣ Keep Success Alive After Delivery

Go‑live is not the end.  
It’s the hand-off to reality — and most orgs stop caring exactly when they should lean in.

Plan value-realisation from day one:

- Name **benefit owners**  
- Schedule check‑ins at 3 / 6 / 12 months  
- Keep measurement simple  
- Plan a **1.1 release** early — the business won’t know what it really needs until they’ve lived with it

And look after your team; burned‑out humans kill benefits faster than bad metrics.

As Anh Dao Pham says: *the glue hardens after delivery.*

## 6️⃣ Red Flags You’re Chasing the Wrong Definition

- Everything’s green, nobody’s smiling  
- “Value” ends at the deployment report  
- Execs reset the target every review  
- Benefits “belong to the business,” but no one can name them  

When you spot these, pause — don’t push.  
Renegotiate success.

## The WorkFriend Definition

> **Project success is delivering meaningful, measurable value through people who still believe in the work when the Gantt chart is done.**  

It sits at the intersection of **clarity**, **courage**, and **connection**.

And when all three align, you’ll hear the magic words:

> “Stacey kept us organised — and it was easy and smooth.”

## Quick Reference Glossary

| Term | Plain Definition | Why It Matters |
|------|------------------|----------------|
| **Line‑of‑Sight** | Linking daily work to long‑term strategy | Prevents isolated busywork |
| **Value‑in‑Use** | Benefit the user experiences, not the feature delivered | Distinguishes output from impact |
| **Benefits Realisation** | Tracking whether promised advantages appear | Extends accountability past go‑live |
| **Management‑for‑Stakeholders** | Making decisions around how each group benefits | Builds trust and alignment |
