# Resistance Mechanics — Inference Card (HBR Core)

## Use When
You’re diagnosing **why people are pushing back**, stalling, or avoiding change — visibly or quietly.  
Use this card to identify **root drivers**, not just symptoms.

## Canonical Truths (from book extracts)
- Resistance is **not defiance — it is protection** (competence, identity, status, belonging).
- The most powerful force in change is the human need to **belong and remain valued**.
- People resist **loss of control**, not change itself.
- Ambiguity forces the brain into **threat mode** → reduced capacity + defensive behaviour.
- Lack of clarity creates a vacuum → **people fill gaps with worst-case stories**.
- Hidden objections grow if **the voice of change isn't surfaced and addressed**.

## Primary Mechanisms of Resistance (root patterns)

| Driver | What it sounds like | What it *actually* means |
|---|---|---|
| **Loss of competence** | “I don’t have time to learn this” | Fear of looking slow or stupid |
| **Loss of identity/status** | “This is how we’ve always done it” | My expertise may not matter anymore |
| **Loss of control/autonomy** | “We weren’t consulted” | I feel change is *being done to me* |
| **Workload increase** | “This will create more work” | Overload + cognitive bandwidth saturation |
| **Mistrust** | “Leadership isn’t telling us everything” | Low safety → avoidance or passive resistance |
| **Ambiguity** | “What exactly do you want us to do?” | Threat response to unclear expectations |

Use this card to diagnose behaviour **before intervening**.

## Behavioural Signals (overt + covert)
Overt resistance:
- Pushback in meetings, heated debate
- Challenging decisions publicly
- “Why are we doing this now?”

Covert resistance:
- Quiet compliance but no follow-through
- Slow-walking tasks
- Private cynicism / satire
- “We’ll wait it out — change never sticks here”

Micro-behaviours:
- Cameras off, short answers
- Avoiding ownership
- Excessive clarification questions as **stall tactic**

## Recommended Plays / Intervention Patterns

### 1. Surface the Real Objection
> “What part feels risky or costly from your perspective?”  
> “If this goes wrong, what’s the feared scenario?”  

*Goal:* Convert **silent resistance → voiced reality**.

---

### 2. Re-establish agency (control restores safety)
> “Here are two ways we could approach this — which feels more workable?”  
> “What would make this 10% easier?”

Even small choices reduce threat.

---

### 3. Separate emotion from implementation
> “First let’s understand how this feels — then we’ll work out the plan.”

People can’t solution while dysregulated.

---

### 4. Address competency threat gently
> “It’s normal to feel unsteady when learning something new — nobody is expected to be perfect immediately.”

Pair with scaffolded change rollout.

---

### 5. Reduce ambiguity fast
Answer three questions every time:
- What’s changing?
- Why now?
- What will be different for *me*?

If you can’t answer → **don’t roll yet**.

---

## Anti-Patterns (things that worsen resistance)
❌ Flooding people with logic instead of addressing emotion  
❌ “Just get on board” messaging  
❌ Pretending certainty when unknowns exist  
❌ Announcing change and disappearing  
❌ Presenting glossy decks instead of listening sessions  
❌ Overloading people with too many initiatives  

Resistance is a **feedback signal**, not sabotage.

---

## Good Outcomes Look Like
- People voice concerns earlier and more openly
- Debate shifts from personal → problem/solution space
- Individuals feel heard and regain autonomy
- Friction moves from emotional → operational
- Uptake becomes voluntary rather than forced

When this card works, resistance changes from **silent drag → engaged dialogue**.

---

## Quick Guidance for WAI handling queries
If user asks about pushback/resistance, WAI should:

1. Ask what *type* of resistance they’re seeing (overt/covert/micro).
2. Probe gently for fear source → **competence / identity / control / workload / trust / ambiguity**.
3. Offer one intervention play tailored to the driver.
4. Ask: *“Do you want a script, root cause map, or a tactical action plan?”*
5. Never assume the cause — always confirm.
