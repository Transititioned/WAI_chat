# Communication Patterns — Inference Card (HBR Core)

## Use When
You need to **explain change clearly, reduce anxiety**, or design a message that will actually land with humans (not just transfer information).  
Use this when writing comms, scripts, kickoff decks, talking points, FAQs, or preparing leadership messaging.

## Canonical Truths (grounded in book extracts)
- Under change, **emotions lead — logic follows once people feel safe**.
- Clarity lowers threat response. **Ambiguity breeds fear → rumours → resistance**.
- People remember **what affects them personally**, not generic strategy.
- Messages must hit **three channels**: emotional → rational → actionable.
- Repetition is necessary — when emotions spike, **processing drops ~80%**.
- People want to know:  
  **What is changing? Why now? How does it affect me? What do I do? What stays the same?**

This card gives you templates to answer those questions efficiently.

---

## Core Message Structures

### 1. **Heart → Head → Hands**
A universal change communication sequence.

**Heart (emotion)**  
Acknowledge feelings & uncertainty. Show care.  
> “This change may feel unsettling and that’s normal — many of us are in the same space.”

**Head (logic & context)**  
Give purpose, rationale, and consequences of not changing.  
> “Our current process takes 12 days and causes backlog. This new approach brings it down to 3.”

**Hands (action & clarity)**  
What happens next & what people must do.  
> “This week we’re piloting with Team A. You’ll get login instructions Friday. Your part is just step 1.”

*If any layer is missing → friction increases.*

---

### 2. **From → To transformation statements**
Used to make change **concrete and relatable**.

Template:
> “We are moving **from** X (current behaviour) **to** Y (new behaviour/outcome), so that Z (value case).”

Examples:
- From individual spreadsheets → to shared dashboard → so decisions are aligned.
- From escalation firefighting → to preventative planning → so workload becomes sane.

This framing **reduces ambiguity + signals what changes vs. what remains stable**.

---

### 3. The *Why–What–Impact* Frame
Especially good for frontline teams.

> Why – The reason and urgency  
> What – What changes in practice  
> Impact – What it means for *me*

Example:
> “We’re updating rostering to reduce double-bookings (why).  
> You’ll now request shift swaps in the app instead of email (what).  
> That means faster approvals and fewer weekend surprises (impact).”

---

## Clarity Scaffolding Tools
Simple design choices that prevent overload and misinterpretation.

- Use **simple, concrete language** — no strategy fog.
- Keep messages to **three key points max** (five is upper bound).
- Visually separate **facts vs decisions vs unknowns**.
- When things are uncertain, **say so directly and promise an update date**.
- Reinforce continuity: **“Here’s what isn’t changing.”**
- Repeat core messages **across formats**: verbal, written, visual, example story.

---

## Behavioural Signals Your Comms Aren’t Landing
Look for these in meetings or the hallway:

- Questions like: *“Wait — what’s changing exactly?”*
- Teams paraphrasing the change incorrectly.
- People waiting passively rather than acting.
- Whispered “What does this mean for our jobs?”
- Leaders saying “I told them already” (frequency ≠ absorption)

**Signal rule:**  
If you need to *explain the explanation*, the message is too complex.

## Sample Delivery Scripts (short + live-usable)

**Kickoff meeting**
> “Some of us are excited, some unsure — all normal.  
> Here’s why this matters now.  
> Here’s what will look different in your day.  
> First step is small — we try this for two weeks and learn together.”

**When people fear loss**
> “It’s okay not to feel ready. We’re moving one small step at a time.  
> Let’s talk about what support you’d need to feel confident.”

**When teams misinterpret change**
> “Let’s rewrite this together as a clear *From → To → So that*.”

**When resistance is emotional**
> “Tell me what part feels hard — is it clarity, workload, uncertainty, or something else?”

---

## Quick Guidance for WAI Usage

When user asks for communication help, WAI should:

1. Ask whether the message is for **announcement / alignment / resistance / rollout / exec deck / frontline crew**.
2. Offer **two structure options** (Heart-Head-Hands OR From→To).
3. Ask: **“Do you want a draft message, a meeting script, or a one-liner framing?”**
4. Provide an example with **their context embedded**.
5. Never default to generic emails — ask for audience + emotional state first.
