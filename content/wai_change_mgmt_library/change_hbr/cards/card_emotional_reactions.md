# Emotional Reactions to Change — Inference Card (HBR Core)

## Use When
People are struggling emotionally with change. You need to understand or respond to fear, confusion, overwhelm, defensiveness, or disengagement.

## Canonical Truths (from book extracts)
- Humans react to change as a **threat response**.
- Uncertainty consumes **extra neural energy**, reducing capacity to think clearly.
- Under stress, **information processing drops up to 80%**.
- Emotional reactions are **predictable, normal, and not resistance by default**.
- Loss of competence or status creates **fear + protection behaviours**.
- Change fatigue appears when pace/volume > capacity → **withdrawal, sarcasm, burnout**.
- Early emotional turbulence is **not a sign of failure — it’s phase-appropriate**.

## Pattern Recognition Cues
Look for:
- Silence, blank stares, low participation
- Jokes in backchannels / cynicism
- “This is just another change…” fatigue statements
- Overwork, slowed work rate, small mistakes
- Tight body language (crossed arms, downcast eyes)
- “What will happen to me?” questions
- Defensive expertise (“I’ve been doing this 20 years…”)

Red flags:
- Anxiety → urgency to “fix everything now”
- Fight/flight/freeze behaviours
- Hidden resistance disguised as compliance
- Emotional contagion inside teams

## Recommended Plays / Scripts

**Acknowledge + Normalize**
> “It makes sense this feels uncomfortable — change drains energy and breaks routine.”

**Name feelings safely**
> “Sounds like there’s uncertainty here. What part feels unclear or risky from your side?”

**Reveal stability**
> “Here’s what *isn’t* changing — your role remains valued, your expertise matters.”

**Reduce threat perception**
> “You won’t be expected to be perfect immediately — we’ll learn this gradually.”

**Shift from emotion → sense-making**
> “If we slow down for a moment — what part feels the most stressful? What would make it easier?”

**Diffuse overwhelm**
> “Let’s move one step at a time. What’s the smallest next move?”

## What to Avoid
- Flooding with logic/data when the audience is still emotional
- Telling people to “get on board”
- Pretending certainty when ambiguity exists
- Minimizing feelings: “It’s not that bad, relax”
- Overloading with instructions during anxiety states

## Good Outcomes Look Like
- People ask curious + constructive questions
- Concerns are spoken openly, not whispered privately
- Emotional temperature decreases
- Individuals regain sense of choice/control
- Team shifts from threat → problem-solving orientation

## Quick Guidance for WAI handling queries
When a user asks about fear/friction:
1. Validate feelings first (“makes sense…”)
2. Clarify emotional source (“what part feels risky?”)
3. Provide small safe next steps (chunk action)
4. Offer options not commands (choice restores control)
5. Ask if they want **script / example / intervention plan**

Always offer:
> “Do you want a script, a behavioural pattern to use, or practical next actions?”
