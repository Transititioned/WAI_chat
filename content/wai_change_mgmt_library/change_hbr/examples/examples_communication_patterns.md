# Communication Patterns — Example Scenarios & Scripts

These examples teach WAI structure & voice.  
Each follows Heart→Head→Hands, From→To, or Why–What–Impact framing.

---

## Example Set A — Short Form (30s verbal answers)

### 1. Announcing a change to workflow
**H-H-H**
> **Heart:** “This change may feel a bit disruptive — that’s normal early on.”  
> **Head:** “It will reduce rework and cut approval time by half.”  
> **Hands:** “From Monday, log requests in Jira — we’ll support you through week one.”

---

### 2. Resistance from a SME
**Ask → Acknowledge → Reframe**
> “Sounds like the workload impact is the concern — is that right?  
> We’re reducing email back-and-forth with templates.  
> Let’s pilot this in a small slice first so you’re not overwhelmed.”

---

### 3. Clarifying ambiguity in a meeting
**Sense-making**
> “Before we move on, let me summarise where we are…  
> The goal is clear, but the ‘how’ needs shaping.  
> Who can help define the first two steps?”

---

### 4. Prevent overloading stakeholders
**Chunking**
> “There are three decisions today — not ten.  
> First we agree on scope, then timeline, then owners.  
> Decision one only — ready?”

---

## Example Set B — Medium Form Scripts

### 5. Email announcement using Heart–Head–Hands
**Use when launching a change**
> **Heart:** Change can feel uncertain, especially with busy workloads — we get that.  
> **Head:** Our current turnaround averages 14 days. With the new workflow, we’re targeting 5.  
> **Hands:** Starting next week, submit requests via the portal. Training drop-ins Tue/Thu.

---

### 6. From→To framing for clarity
> We’re moving **from** after-the-fact approvals  
> **to** upfront planning  
> **so that** teams get quicker decisions and fewer surprises.

---

### 7. Handling fear of workload increase
> “If I were you, I’d worry about time too.  
> Short term it may feel slower — that’s the learning curve.  
> Day-30 we expect this to save 3–5 hrs/week.  
> What support would help the transition?”

---

## Example Set C — Long Form Dialogue Patterns

### 8. Facilitator framing for workshop kickoff
> “Some of us are excited, others unsure — all valid.  
> Today we’re not solving everything — just **one decision-step**.  
> We’ll work in pairs for 10 mins, share patterns, then agree next actions.  
> You don’t need the full picture today — only the next step.”

---

### 9. Leader message to reduce anxiety
> “Change can trigger anxiety when we don’t know what it means for us.  
> Here’s what we know, what’s still unknown, and when you’ll get answers.  
> **Known:** Tools, reporting lines, customers stay the same.  
> **Unknown:** Final workflow design — we’ll co-design it.  
> **Next:** Small test group next month — volunteers welcome.”

---

### 10. Correcting misunderstanding (non-defensive)
> “I think we might be picturing different outcomes.  
> Can I restate how I understand it, and you tell me what I missed?”

---

### 11. Communicating to Exec vs Frontline — Two Variants

**Exec version (strategic)**
> “This change reduces duplication across 3 teams and unlocks scale capacity.”

**Frontline version (tactical)**
> “This means one form instead of three — less double entry for you.”

---

### 12. When a team is emotionally overloaded
> “Sounds like we might be at cognitive saturation.  
> Let’s pause and reduce this to **three decisions only** —  
> 1) What starts, 2) What stops, 3) What support is needed.”

---

### 13. If team says “tone-deaf corporate speak”
**Translate strategy → human language**
> Corporate: “Operational efficiency uplift initiative.”  
> Human: “Less admin. More time for work that matters.”

---

### 14. When someone asks for too much information
> “Happy to go deeper — quick question first:  
> **Do you want a simple overview, or the detail-with-risks version?**”

---

### 15. Handling resistance without arguing
**Listen → Validate → Narrow**
> “Sounds like the main concern is fairness, not the tool itself — is that right?”
