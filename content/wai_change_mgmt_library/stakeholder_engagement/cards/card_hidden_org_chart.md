# Hidden organisation chart

## Trigger

The project has a formal stakeholder map, but new stakeholders keep appearing late or decisions keep being influenced by people who were not included.

You may hear:

- “You should really talk to Jenny before deciding that.”
- “Finance will have a view on this.”
- “The warehouse team will not accept that.”
- “That process is actually managed by someone else.”
- “We need to check with the person who owns the workaround.”

## What it may mean

The visible stakeholder map is missing the organisation’s working network.

The project may have mapped reporting lines, but not:

- process ownership
- practical knowledge
- informal approval paths
- historical project scars
- local workarounds
- people who interpret decisions for others
- people who can make the change credible

## What to do next

Build the hidden organisation chart iteratively.

Start with the known sponsor and formal stakeholders. For each conversation, ask who else:

- has expertise
- will be impacted
- might disagree
- has to change behaviour
- has lived through previous attempts
- is trusted by the team
- understands the exceptions

Then review the names for patterns. If the same person is mentioned repeatedly, they likely matter.

## What not to do

Do not treat stakeholder identification as a once-off initiation task.

Do not assume “not senior” means “not important”.

Do not wait until UAT or training to discover that the wrong people were involved in design or validation.

## Useful workplace language

“I’m building out the practical stakeholder view. Who else would you include if we want this to land properly?”

“Who might be affected by this even if they are not in the formal project structure?”

“Who has the process history we need before we make assumptions?”

## Artefact or meeting move

Use a stakeholder snowballing table.

| Starting stakeholder | People they nominated | Reason nominated | Follow-up priority | Pattern observed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
|  |  | Impacted / expert / influencer / approver / sceptic | High / Medium / Low |  |

Review after 5–10 conversations and look for repeated names.

## ERP / technology delivery example

A project may begin with functional leads for Sales, Warehouse, Finance, and Purchasing. During discovery, the team learns that several exceptions are managed by long-serving coordinators who are not in the project structure.

Those people should be engaged before SOPs and test cases are treated as stable.
