# Change role trifecta

## Trigger

The project expects the PM, BA, or change person to “manage the change,” but the influence structure is weak.

You may notice:

- the sponsor is nominal but not visible
- there are no credible business champions
- the project has communications but no local advocacy
- managers are not reinforcing expectations
- users do not know who to trust for practical guidance
- change actions are disconnected from testing, SOPs, training, and support

## What it may mean

The change model is underpowered.

A change plan alone cannot compensate for missing sponsorship, missing local credibility, or poor integration across delivery activities.

## What to do next

Check whether three roles are covered:

1. **Change sponsor** — provides visible authority, prioritisation, and executive backing.
2. **Change champions** — respected people who can make the change credible locally.
3. **Change integrator** — coordinates readiness, communication, training, impacts, risks, and follow-through.

If a role is missing, name the gap as a delivery risk rather than a personal criticism.

## What not to do

Do not pretend the PM or change lead can substitute for visible sponsorship.

Do not appoint champions who are not respected by the people affected.

Do not treat champions as unpaid project resources without clarifying expectations and manager support.

Do not assume a communication plan equals adoption.

## Useful workplace language

“To make this land, we need three things covered: visible sponsorship, local credibility, and someone joining the dots across readiness, comms, training, and risk.”

“I can coordinate the change activity, but business reinforcement needs to come through the right leaders and local champions.”

“Who would users naturally trust when they ask, ‘Will this actually work for us?’”

## Artefact or meeting move

Create a change role map.

| Role | Person / group | What we need from them | Current strength | Gap / action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sponsor |  | Visible backing, prioritisation, escalation support | Strong / Medium / Weak |  |
| Champions |  | Local credibility, feedback, practical support | Strong / Medium / Weak |  |
| Integrator |  | Join readiness, comms, training, impacts, risks | Strong / Medium / Weak |  |

## ERP / technology delivery example

For an ERP upgrade:

- the sponsor gives authority to prioritise business SME time
- functional leads and respected users provide local credibility
- the PM/change lead integrates SOPs, UAT, training, cutover communication, and readiness tracking

Without all three, the project may have documentation but weak adoption.
