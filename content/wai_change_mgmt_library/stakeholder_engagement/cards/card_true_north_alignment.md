# True north alignment

## Trigger

Stakeholders are pulling the project in different directions.

You may notice:

- each function defines success differently
- scope debates keep reopening
- priorities shift based on who is in the room
- the project team cannot explain the governing business principle
- trade-offs are being made without a stable reference point

## What it may mean

The project does not have a clear “true north” that stakeholders recognise and use.

Without a shared guidepost, decisions become local, political, or reactive. The project may drift from its original purpose or become overloaded with unrelated expectations.

## What to do next

Create a simple true north statement and use it actively.

It should answer:

1. Why is this project being pursued?
2. Which organisational goal, risk, or business outcome does it support?
3. What decisions should this purpose help us make?
4. What does this purpose exclude or de-prioritise?
5. What trade-offs are acceptable?

Use the true north statement at the start of key meetings, governance packs, and scope discussions.

## What not to do

Do not create a polished purpose statement that no one uses.

Do not let every stakeholder redefine the project around their local pain point.

Do not use “alignment” language to shut down valid concerns. Use it to clarify decisions.

## Useful workplace language

“To keep this grounded, can we come back to the main reason this project exists?”

“That may be a valid improvement, but does it support the purpose of this release or should it go into the next-phase backlog?”

“We need a stable guidepost for these trade-offs, otherwise every decision becomes a fresh debate.”

## ERP / technology delivery example

For an ERP upgrade, true north might be:

“Safely move to the supported ERP version, protect business continuity, validate critical customisations, and create a stable platform for later process improvement.”

This gives a practical decision rule:

- must-do: stability, compatibility, critical process continuity
- should-do: SOP and UAT coverage for priority flows
- not-now: broad process redesign unless needed for safe go-live
