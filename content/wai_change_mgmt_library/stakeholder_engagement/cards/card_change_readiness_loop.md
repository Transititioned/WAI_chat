# Change readiness loop

## Trigger

The project has a change plan, but nobody is regularly checking whether the business is actually becoming more ready.

You may notice:

- readiness is discussed only near go-live
- training exists, but confidence is unknown
- stakeholders say they are okay, but actions lag
- adoption risks are scattered across meeting notes
- the business is “informed” but not demonstrably prepared
- readiness is treated as a checklist rather than an evidence-based view

## What it may mean

Change readiness is not being managed as a live delivery loop.

The project may have communication, training, and stakeholder activities, but no regular mechanism for checking whether those activities are improving readiness.

Readiness is not a static state. It changes as the solution, process, timing, training, support model, and business pressure change.

## What to do next

Create a repeating readiness loop:

1. **Articulate** what readiness means for this release.
2. **Define** the areas where change must be managed.
3. **Assess** readiness, risk, resistance, capacity, and confidence.
4. **Plan** actions to close the gaps.
5. **Take action** and then reassess.

Run the loop at a regular cadence. For a major delivery, fortnightly may be useful during planning and weekly near go-live.

## What not to do

Do not assume that sending communications means stakeholders are ready.

Do not wait until go/no-go to discover readiness gaps.

Do not rely only on optimistic verbal updates.

Do not treat change management as a document set rather than a feedback loop.

## Useful workplace language

“We do not need a heavy change machine, but we do need a regular read on where the business is confident, exposed, or unclear.”

“Let’s make readiness visible now, rather than discovering the gaps at go-live.”

“I want to separate informed from ready. People may know about the change but still not be ready to operate it.”

## Artefact or meeting move

Create a readiness loop tracker.

|  | Process / system / role / training / support | Green / Amber / Red |  |  |  |  |

## ERP / technology delivery example

For an ERP upgrade, readiness should be reassessed as:

- the environment stabilises
- customisations are validated
- SOPs are drafted
- UAT proves the happy path
- training materials become real
- support arrangements are confirmed
- cutover dates become firm

A one-time readiness review near go-live is too late to manage this well.
