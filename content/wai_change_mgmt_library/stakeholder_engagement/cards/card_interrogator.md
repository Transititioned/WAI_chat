# The Interrogator

## Trigger

A specific stakeholder consistently asks aggressive, detailed, awkward, or high-pressure questions in governance meetings.

Typical signs:

- They read the pack closely and find weak assumptions.
- Their questions slow or derail approval.
- Other people wait to see whether they object.
- The project team starts preparing defensively for their questions.

## What it may mean

The stakeholder may be protecting their function, testing the quality of the thinking, managing personal accountability, carrying prior project scar tissue, or surfacing risk the project has underplayed.

They are not necessarily an enemy. They may be the best available early warning system for the questions that will matter in the room.

## What to do next

1. Identify the likely challenger before the meeting.
2. Give them the draft pack early.
3. Set up a short one-on-one or informal review.
4. Ask what stands out, what feels under-supported, and what would make the decision easier.
5. Capture their concerns in a governance challenge log.
6. Update the pack where the challenge is legitimate.
7. In the meeting, acknowledge their input rather than treating them as a disruption.

## What not to do

Do not try to shut them down publicly.

Do not label them as difficult too quickly. If their questions expose weak assumptions, the problem is not the question; it is the readiness of the material.

## Useful workplace language

“I know you tend to look closely at these packs, which is useful. Before the PCG, I’d value your read on whether anything looks unclear or under-supported.”

“What would you need to see to feel comfortable with this recommendation?”

“You’ve got a good eye for where this could get challenged. Can I test the logic with you before it goes wider?”

## ERP / technology delivery example

If a finance, audit, risk, or operations leader repeatedly challenges ERP upgrade dates or assumptions, meet them before governance. Ask what evidence would make the plan supportable. The goal is not to avoid scrutiny; it is to improve the plan and avoid preventable public friction.
