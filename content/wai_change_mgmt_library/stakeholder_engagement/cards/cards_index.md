# Stakeholder Engagement Cards Index

This folder contains trigger/action cards for diagnosing and responding to stakeholder, governance, sponsor, influence, and readiness situations.

The cards are designed for chatbot retrieval. Each card follows a practical pattern:

```text
Trigger → What it may mean → What to do next → What not to do → Useful language → Artefact / meeting move → ERP / technology example
```

## Common response pattern for chatbot use

When a user describes a governance, sponsor-control, hidden-influence, stakeholder-discovery, purpose, expectation, business-case, trust, communication, stakeholder-behaviour, readiness, training, go-live confidence, post-launch learning, BAU transition, or project-closure issue, respond in this structure:

1. **What you are seeing**
   - Name the likely pattern.
   - Mention the observable trigger.
   - If relevant, distinguish silence, scepticism, or surprise from true agreement or resistance.
   - If relevant, distinguish technical readiness from operational readiness.
   - If relevant, distinguish go-live completion from post-launch stabilisation or BAU ownership.

2. **What it may mean**
   - Explain the stakeholder, influence, governance, purpose, scope, trust, communication, readiness, training, post-launch, BAU transition, or delivery dynamic.
   - Avoid assuming bad intent.
   - Distinguish formal authority from practical influence where relevant.
   - Distinguish technical output from business outcome where relevant.
   - Treat scepticism as possible project memory, not automatic negativity.
   - Treat training requests as possible signals of process, role, system, or support uncertainty.
   - Treat post-launch fatigue as a risk to organisational learning, not a reason to skip the learning loop.

3. **What to do next**
   - Give concrete actions.
   - Include pre-briefing, socialisation, trade-off framing, challenger engagement, stakeholder discovery, influence mapping, purpose clarification, business-case testing, expectation alignment, trust follow-through, active agreement checks, early risk updates, readiness loops, operational readiness checks, role mapping, training triage, must/should/nice classification, post-launch review, blame-safe lessons learned, or BAU transition planning where relevant.

4. **What to say**
   - Provide practical workplace language.
   - Keep the tone calm, respectful, and delivery-focused.
   - Avoid manipulative or overly political language.
   - When challenging weak rationale, unrealistic constraints, stakeholder assumptions, readiness claims, or closure assumptions, make it about strengthening delivery confidence and operational ownership, not attacking the sponsor or stakeholder.

5. **Artefact or meeting move**
   - Recommend a sponsor pre-brief note, governance challenge log, decision socialisation map, timeline trade-off table, stakeholder discovery log, power-interest map, informal influencer engagement note, purpose clarity card, red-face test one-pager, scope/expectation alignment table, true north map, problem-to-solution chain, risk communication drumbeat, project memory risk log, active agreement check, pushback options table, trust follow-through tracker, readiness loop tracker, change role map, operational readiness checklist, training readiness triage table, go-live finish table, post-launch mirror board, blame-safe lessons table, or BAU transition matrix.

6. **ERP / technology delivery adaptation**
   - Translate the advice into a realistic ERP, platform, system upgrade, testing, vendor, process, training, business-readiness, cutover, hypercare, reporting, support model, BAU ownership, or improvement-backlog context.

## Notes

This index should be updated after each card batch is added.

Keep the index practical. It should help the chatbot understand when to retrieve each card, not merely list file names.

Use neutral language for hidden influence patterns. The aim is to help the user engage respectfully and improve delivery, not to create a political dossier.

Use careful language for weak business-case patterns. The aim is to strengthen purpose, scope, and decision quality, not to embarrass sponsors or dismiss ideas too early.

Use respectful language for trust and resistance patterns. The aim is to surface delivery risks and project memory, not to label stakeholders as difficult.

Use evidence-based language for readiness patterns. The aim is to separate optimism from operational confidence and to make readiness gaps visible early.

Use practical language for post-launch patterns. The aim is to capture reusable learning, separate defects from enhancements, and transition ownership safely without turning closure into blame or abandonment.
