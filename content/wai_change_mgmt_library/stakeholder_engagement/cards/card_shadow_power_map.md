# Shadow power map

## Trigger

The person with the formal title is not the person others seem to follow.

You may notice:

- a senior stakeholder agrees but nothing moves
- a lower-level person’s opinion changes the room
- people defer to someone who is not on the governance list
- the real discussion happens before or after the formal meeting
- decisions are revisited after “checking with” someone else

## What it may mean

Formal authority and practical influence are not aligned.

The person with the authority to approve may still rely on someone else for confidence, technical detail, process legitimacy, or team acceptance.

This is common in long-tenured business areas, ERP environments, operational teams, and functions where local knowledge is more trusted than project language.

## What to do next

Create a shadow power map.

Map both:

1. **Formal authority** — who can approve, fund, prioritise, or assign people.
2. **Practical influence** — who others listen to, consult, trust, or follow.

Then decide how to engage each person respectfully.

Questions to ask:

- Who needs to approve this formally?
- Who needs to believe this is workable?
- Who will others look to before they accept the change?
- Who can quietly slow this down if they are not brought in early?
- Who has the practical history?

## What not to do

Do not bypass formal authority. That can create political risk.

Do not flatter informal influencers in a way that threatens their manager.

Do not label people as “shadow power” in shared artefacts. Use neutral language such as “influence”, “process knowledge”, “adoption role”, or “engagement priority”.

## Useful workplace language

“I can see the team really values your practical view on this. It would help to get your input before we lock in the approach.”

“Who else should we involve so the solution is grounded in how the work actually happens?”

“I want to make sure we have both the formal decision path and the practical process knowledge covered.”

## Artefact or meeting move

Create a private stakeholder influence map.

| Person / role | Formal authority | Practical influence | Why they matter | Engagement approach |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
|  | High / Medium / Low | High / Medium / Low | Decision / process knowledge / adoption / risk |  |

Keep sensitive influence notes private. Do not publish political interpretations into project-wide spaces.

## ERP / technology delivery example

An ERP team may get formal sign-off from the operations manager, but the warehouse team will not fully engage until a trusted senior supervisor accepts the testing approach.

The right move is not to undermine the manager. It is to involve the supervisor as a practical reviewer while keeping the manager informed and respected.
