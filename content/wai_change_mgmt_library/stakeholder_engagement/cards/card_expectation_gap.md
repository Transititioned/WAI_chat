# Expectation gap

## Trigger

Stakeholders believe the project will deliver more, sooner, or differently than the actual scope supports.

You may hear:

- “I thought this would fix that as well.”
- “Will this remove all manual work?”
- “So the new system will handle the whole process?”
- “Once this goes live, we will not need the workaround anymore, right?”
- “Why is that not included?”

## What it may mean

There is a gap between expected value and delivered value.

The project may be on track against its formal scope but still heading toward perceived failure because stakeholders are carrying a different picture of success.

This is especially risky when the project has a broad label such as “transformation,” “modernisation,” “ERP upgrade,” or “automation.”

## What to do next

Make expectations explicit.

1. State what the project will deliver.
2. State what it will not deliver.
3. Separate confirmed scope from assumptions.
4. Identify benefits that depend on business process, training, data, or adoption.
5. Capture out-of-scope improvements in a backlog rather than letting them float.
6. Repeat the message consistently in governance, workshops, and updates.

## What not to do

Do not rely on the scope document alone.

Do not assume that because the project team understands the scope, stakeholders do too.

Do not let aspirational benefits sound like committed deliverables.

Do not hide exclusions because they feel awkward.

## Useful workplace language

“I want to separate what this release definitely delivers from what it enables later.”

“That is a valid improvement idea, but I do not want to accidentally imply it is part of this go-live scope.”

“Let’s capture that in the improvement backlog so it is not lost, while keeping the current release clear.”

## Artefact or meeting move

Create a scope and expectation alignment table.

## ERP / technology delivery example

Stakeholders may believe an ERP upgrade will automatically improve every painful process.

The project should clarify:

- the upgrade will move the business to a supported version
- customisations will be validated or fixed where required
- SOPs and testing will focus on safe continuity and critical happy paths
- business improvements will be captured for a later prioritised release

This prevents “upgrade” from being heard as “everything gets fixed”.
