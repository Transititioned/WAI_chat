# Post-launch mirror

## Trigger

The project has gone live and everyone wants to move on.

You may notice:

- the team is exhausted
- sponsor attention has moved elsewhere
- users are still adjusting
- hypercare issues are being solved but not analysed
- nobody has time for a proper review
- lessons learned are treated as a final administrative step

## What it may mean

The organisation may be about to lose valuable learning.

Go-live is not the end of the change. It is the first point where the project can see whether the solution, process, training, support model, and stakeholder engagement worked under real conditions.

Without a post-launch mirror, the same delivery pain will be repeated on the next initiative.

## What to do next

Run a short, practical post-launch review focused on reusable learning.

Ask:

1. What worked well enough to reuse?
2. What created avoidable pain?
3. What surprised us after go-live?
4. What did users need that we underestimated?
5. What should be changed in the project method, readiness approach, training, testing, or support model?
6. What should be added to the improvement backlog?
7. What should BAU own from here?

Keep it practical. The aim is not a long report. The aim is better delivery next time.

## What not to do

Do not run the session as a blame exercise.

Do not wait so long that people forget the details.

Do not only invite the project team. Include business, support, vendor, and operational perspectives where relevant.

Do not capture lessons that have no owner, action, or reuse path.

## Useful workplace language

“I know everyone is tired, but this is the point where the real learning is freshest.”

“Let’s keep this practical: what would we repeat, what would we change, and what should we pass to the next team?”

“This is not about blame. It is about avoiding the same pain next time.”

## ERP / technology delivery example

After ERP go-live, the team realises that business users understood the happy path but struggled with exceptions.

The post-launch mirror captures that future ERP releases need:

- exception-based job aids
- named floor support
- earlier UAT scenarios for edge cases
- clearer hypercare triage
