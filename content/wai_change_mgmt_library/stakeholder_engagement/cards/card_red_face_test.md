# The red-face test

## Trigger

A project sounds important, but the rationale is vague.

You may notice:

- the benefit statement is full of buzzwords
- the solution is clearer than the problem
- executives support it, but cannot explain the value consistently
- the project would be awkward to defend under scrutiny
- cost, effort, risk, or timing seem out of proportion to the stated benefit

## What it may mean

The project may not yet have a defensible business case.

It may still be worth doing, but the reasoning is not yet strong enough to carry governance, stakeholder engagement, funding, prioritisation, or delivery trade-offs.

The problem is not necessarily that the idea is bad. The problem is that the logic is not yet clear enough.

## What to do next

Ask whether you can defend the project without embarrassment.

Use questions such as:

1. Would we spend our own money on this?
2. What specific problem are we solving?
3. What business outcome changes if we succeed?
4. What happens if we do nothing?
5. What evidence supports the expected benefit?
6. What assumptions would make the business case fall over?
7. What is the smallest credible version of the initiative?

If the rationale is weak, move the conversation from “approval” to “clarification”.

## What not to do

Do not attack the sponsor’s idea.

Do not say “this is not strategic” as a blunt challenge unless you have the authority and relationship to do so.

Do not force-fit the estimate or benefits to match a preferred number.

Do not proceed as if stakeholder support will magically appear later.

## Useful workplace language

“I’m not challenging the direction. I’m trying to make sure we can defend the rationale clearly when others ask why this matters.”

“To set this up properly, I think we need a sharper answer on the problem, the benefit, and the assumptions.”

“If the board asked why this is worth doing now, what would be our strongest answer?”

## ERP / technology delivery example

A business area asks for a major reporting platform because “everyone needs better dashboards.”

The red-face test finds that only three compliance reports are truly critical for go-live. The project reframes the first release around regulatory reporting and moves broader dashboards into a later improvement backlog.
