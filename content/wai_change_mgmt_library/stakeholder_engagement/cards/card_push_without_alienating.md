# Push without alienating

## Trigger

The project lead needs to challenge something important, but the stakeholder may be sensitive, defensive, senior, or politically exposed.

Examples:

- the timeline is unrealistic
- benefits are overstated
- resources are not committed
- scope is unclear
- assumptions are weak
- the sponsor expects the team to “make it fit”
- the stakeholder reacts defensively to questions

## What it may mean

A valid delivery concern may be at risk of being heard as opposition.

The project lead may need to shift from direct challenge to partnership language, while still protecting delivery integrity.

This is not about avoiding hard conversations. It is about making hard conversations possible.

## What to do next

Use a partnership push.

1. Start with shared intent.
2. Ask for help understanding the rationale.
3. State the delivery risk neutrally.
4. Offer options or a next step.
5. Watch for cues that you are pushing too hard.
6. Pull back and reset if the stakeholder becomes defensive.

Watch for signs such as closed body language, vague answers, irritation, raised tone, or abrupt topic changes.

## What not to do

Do not corner the stakeholder in public.

Do not ask questions in a way that sounds like cross-examination.

Do not win the argument and lose the relationship.

Do not agree to a fantasy plan just to keep the conversation comfortable.

## Useful workplace language

“I want to make sure we are setting this up to succeed, so can I test one assumption with you?”

“I can see the outcome you are aiming for. The constraint I am worried about is the available business effort.”

“I may be missing some context. Can you help me understand how you see the timeline working with the current resource position?”

## ERP / technology delivery example

A sponsor wants SIT to start before the ERP environment is stable.

Instead of saying “that is impossible,” the PM says:

“I understand the need to keep momentum. The risk is that if we start SIT before the environment is validated, we may burn business effort on false defects. We can either hold SIT until validation is complete, or run a technical smoke test first and keep business testers out until the environment is credible.”
