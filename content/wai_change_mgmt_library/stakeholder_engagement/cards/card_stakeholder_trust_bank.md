# Stakeholder trust bank

## Trigger

Stakeholders are cautious, sceptical, disengaged, or slow to support the project.

You may notice:

- people wait for proof before committing
- stakeholders do not believe dates or updates
- business users expect the project to create extra work
- managers do not release people for project activities
- concerns are raised privately rather than openly
- previous commitments have not been followed through

## What it may mean

The project has a low trust balance.

Trust is built through repeated small behaviours, not one persuasive message. Stakeholders watch whether the project does what it says, flags risk early, respects their expertise, and creates mutual value.

## What to do next

Make trust operational.

1. Do what you said you would do.
2. Follow up quickly, even when the answer is incomplete.
3. Avoid surprises.
4. Acknowledge stakeholder expertise.
5. Be clear about what is known and unknown.
6. Show how input was used.
7. Do not over-promise.
8. Fix small irritants quickly where possible.

## What not to do

Do not try to charm your way around delivery gaps.

Do not ask stakeholders for repeated input without showing what happened to it.

Do not hide uncertainty to look confident.

Do not assume trust-building is separate from project management. It is part of delivery control.

## Useful workplace language

“You raised that last week, and we have taken it into the test planning. Here is where it landed.”

“I do not have the final answer yet, but I did not want to leave you wondering. The next update will be Friday.”

“I want to be clear about what we know, what we are still validating, and where your input has changed the approach.”

## Artefact or meeting move

Create a trust follow-through tracker.

| Stakeholder input / concern | Commitment made | Follow-up action | Status | Communicated back? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
|  |  |  | Open / Done / Blocked | Yes / No |

## ERP / technology delivery example

Business SMEs raise concerns that test cases do not reflect real exceptions.

The PM logs the concern, works with the testing lead to add priority scenarios, and then tells the SMEs exactly what changed.

That small loop builds more trust than a generic message saying “your feedback is important.”
