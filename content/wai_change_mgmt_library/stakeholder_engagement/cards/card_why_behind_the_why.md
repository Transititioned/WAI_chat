# The why behind the why

## Trigger

The project is being described mainly as a technical output.

Examples:

- “We are upgrading the ERP.”
- “We are moving to the new platform.”
- “We are implementing the workflow tool.”
- “We are replacing the old system.”
- “We need to get onto the supported version.”

These may be true, but they are not enough to create stakeholder alignment.

## What it may mean

The project may not yet have a clear, memorable business purpose.

People may understand what is being delivered, but not why it matters, what business result should improve, or what risk is being reduced.

When this happens, stakeholders may treat the work as an IT activity, compliance chore, or background project rather than a business change.

## What to do next

Ask the “why behind the why” until the business outcome is clear.

Useful questions:

1. Why are we doing this now?
2. What gets better if this succeeds?
3. What risk reduces if this succeeds?
4. What pain continues if we do nothing?
5. Which business outcome should we be able to explain in plain language?
6. Who benefits, and how would they notice?

Turn the answer into a short purpose statement that can be explained in under 30 seconds.

## What not to do

Do not confuse the solution with the reason for the project.

Do not let the project purpose remain as “implement the system” unless the audience is purely technical and the business outcome is already well understood.

Do not invent motivational language that is not supported by the actual business case.

## Useful workplace language

“I understand the system change. I’m trying to get clearer on the business win behind it.”

“If someone from the business asks why this matters, what is the plain-English answer we want to give?”

“Before we go too far into the plan, I want to make sure we have the purpose clear enough that people can repeat it.”

## ERP / technology delivery example

Instead of saying, “We are upgrading the ERP to version 780,” the project might say:

“We are moving to the supported ERP version so we reduce operational risk, confirm critical customisations still work, and give the business a stable platform for process improvement after go-live.”

That statement gives stakeholders a reason to care beyond the technical version number.
