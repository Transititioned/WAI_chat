# Operational readiness gut-check

## Trigger

The solution is technically built or close to built, but the business does not feel ready to operate it.

You may notice:

- users seem nervous about go-live
- training has occurred but confidence is low
- SOPs are unfinished or untested
- support paths are unclear
- old workarounds are still active
- business owners hesitate when asked to confirm readiness
- technical teams say “it works” while operations ask “what happens Monday?”

## What it may mean

The project may be confusing technical readiness with operational readiness.

A system can pass technical testing while the business is not ready to operate safely, consistently, or confidently.

Operational readiness usually needs a view across:

- people
- process
- technology
- data
- support
- culture / confidence
- governance and decision ownership

## What to do next

Run an operational readiness gut-check.

Ask:

1. Do users know what changes for them?
2. Are SOPs or job aids available for critical tasks?
3. Has UAT proven the priority flows?
4. Are roles and handoffs clear?
5. Are support paths known?
6. Are old processes, workarounds, or reports being switched off or controlled?
7. Is there a hypercare plan?
8. Does each business area have a readiness owner?

## What not to do

Do not assume that passing a smoke test means the business is ready.

Do not treat training attendance as evidence of competence.

Do not push for go-live if the business cannot explain how it will operate on day one.

Do not let readiness be judged only by the technical team.

## Useful workplace language

“We know the system can run. I want to check whether the business can operate it safely on Monday morning.”

“Can we separate technical readiness from operational readiness?”

“If I am an end user on day one, do I know what my first three steps are and where to go if something fails?”

## ERP / technology delivery example

An ERP upgrade passes technical validation, but the warehouse team still does not know what to do if scanner transactions fail during picking.

The readiness action is not more generic training. It may be a one-page exception guide, named floor support, and a day-one escalation path.
