# Lessons learned without blame

## Trigger

A lessons learned session is needed, but the team is defensive, tired, or worried the review will become political.

You may hear:

- “We all know what went wrong.”
- “There is no point; nothing changes.”
- “This will become a blame session.”
- “People will just protect themselves.”
- “Let’s not reopen it.”

## What it may mean

The organisation may want learning but not have a safe structure for it.

Without facilitation, lessons learned can become:

- blame
- vague venting
- self-protection
- generic improvement statements
- a document nobody uses

The goal is to turn experience into reusable delivery knowledge.

## What to do next

Set the frame before the session.

Use rules such as:

1. Focus on process, conditions, and decisions, not personal criticism.
2. Separate facts from interpretations.
3. Ask what we would change next time.
4. Capture both strengths and pain points.
5. Convert lessons into method updates, checklist changes, backlog items, or ownership actions.
6. Close the loop on which lessons will actually be used.

## What not to do

Do not ask “Who caused this?”

Do not let the loudest voices dominate.

Do not capture lessons as vague statements like “communicate better.”

Do not run lessons learned if no one will own any resulting actions.

## Useful workplace language

“Let’s keep this about the system of work, not personal fault.”

“The useful question is not ‘who dropped this?’ but ‘what condition allowed this to happen, and what would we change next time?’”

“If a lesson does not change a checklist, plan, role, artefact, or decision point, it probably will not be reused.”

## Artefact or meeting move

Use a blame-safe lessons table.

| Observation | What happened | Contributing condition | Future change | Owner / repository |
|---|---|---|---|---|
|  | Fact-based description | Process / role / timing / tool / decision / capacity |  |  |

## ERP / technology delivery example

After ERP go-live, several defects were found late.

A blame-based review asks: “Why did testing miss this?”

A learning-based review asks:

- Were the right scenarios in the test pack?
- Were the right SMEs available?
- Did the test environment match real use?
- Were customisations mapped clearly enough?
- What should change in the next test planning checklist?
