# Example: unclear business case and the red-face test

## Situation

A sponsor proposes a new digital tool.

The proposed benefit is described as:

“It will modernise the way we work and improve collaboration.”

The idea may be good, but the business case is not yet defensible.

## Pattern

This is a red-face test issue.

The language sounds positive, but it is too vague to support prioritisation, funding, stakeholder engagement, or delivery trade-offs.

## Practical response

The delivery lead does not attack the idea. They strengthen the rationale.

Questions:

- What specific collaboration problem are we solving?
- Which teams are affected?
- How often does the problem occur?
- What does it cost us in time, risk, rework, or delay?
- What happens if we do nothing this year?
- What would be the smallest useful release?
- What evidence would convince Finance or Operations?

## Reframed business case

Weak version:

“Implement a collaboration platform to modernise work.”

Stronger version:

“Reduce handover delays between operations and finance by creating one controlled workflow for exception approvals, replacing email-based tracking for the top three high-volume exception types.”

## Useful workplace language

“I think the idea has merit. I just want to sharpen the problem statement so the business case is easier to defend.”

“If we put this in front of governance, I think they will ask what specific pain it removes. Let’s answer that before they ask.”

## Good outcome

The project either becomes more focused and defensible, or the organisation realises the idea needs more discovery before funding a full implementation.
