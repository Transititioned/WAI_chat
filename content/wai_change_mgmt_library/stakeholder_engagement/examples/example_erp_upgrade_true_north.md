# ERP example: true north for a version upgrade

## Situation

The project is described as:

“We are upgrading the ERP from the old version to the new version.”

That is accurate, but it is not enough to align stakeholders. Different groups are hearing different things:

- IT hears platform currency and supportability.
- Operations hears disruption and testing workload.
- Finance hears reporting and controls risk.
- Business users hear “maybe this fixes our process pain.”
- Executives hear cost, risk, and timing.

## Pattern

This is a true north alignment issue.

The project needs a purpose statement that explains the business reason, decision logic, and scope boundary.

## Better framing

A practical true north statement:

“Safely move to the supported ERP version, protect business continuity, validate critical customisations, and create a stable platform for later process improvement.”

## How this helps decisions

| Decision area | True north implication |
|---|---|
| Customisations | Validate or fix what is needed for safe continuity before adding new improvement scope. |
| SOPs | Focus first on critical happy paths and known operational risks. |
| Testing | Prioritise business-critical flows, integration points, and customisation impacts. |
| Training | Train users on what changes for safe operation, not every theoretical feature. |
| Improvement ideas | Capture in backlog unless required for safe go-live. |

## Useful workplace language

“The upgrade is the technical activity. The business purpose is safe continuity on a supported platform.”

“We should capture improvement ideas, but this release needs to stay anchored to safe go-live and critical process validation.”

## Good outcome

Stakeholders can still raise improvements, but the project has a clear basis for deciding what belongs in the current release and what belongs in a later improvement backlog.
